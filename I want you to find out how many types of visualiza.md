# I want you to find out how many types of visualization techniques are there in football analytics.

There isn’t one fixed universal count, but a common football/sports analytics breakdown gives **9 visualization techniques**: line charts, bar graphs, pie charts, donut charts, scatter plots, heat maps, dashboards, motion charts, and widgets.[^1_1]

## What that means

This count comes from a sports analytics source that explicitly lists those nine techniques for analyzing sports data, including football-related use cases like tracking player performance, comparing teams, and showing movement patterns.[^1_1]

## Important nuance

If you count broader visualization categories used in football analytics, the number can be higher because people also include things like maps, box plots, treemaps, and shot maps depending on the analyst and toolset. So the safest answer is: **at least 9 commonly cited techniques in sports analytics, and more if you use a broader data-viz definition**.[^1_2][^1_3][^1_1]

## Football-specific examples

In football analytics, heat maps are often used for player positioning and movement, scatter plots for relationships like shots vs goals, and dashboards for combining match and player metrics in one view.[^1_1]

With anonymized **tracking + event data** for a single football match, you can still uncover a lot: team shape, pressing behavior, passing patterns, shot quality, player workload, spacing, transitions, and set-piece tendencies.[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3]
Even without player names, the identities are usually less important than the **roles**, movements, and interactions on the pitch.[^3_4][^3_5]

## What you can analyze

- Team shape in possession and out of possession, including width, depth, compactness, and line heights.[^3_6][^3_1]
- Pressing style, such as high press, mid-block, low block, and passes allowed per defensive action (PPDA)-style pressure intensity.[^3_3]
- Player movement metrics, including distance, speed, acceleration, sprint distance, and load intensity.[^3_7][^3_8]
- Passing structure, such as passing networks, central vs wide build-up, progression chains, and ball circulation patterns.[^3_5][^3_9]
- Attacking quality, including shot locations, shot maps, shot sequences, expected goals (xG), and chance creation zones.[^3_10][^3_5]
- Defensive actions, like recoveries, interceptions, clearances, tackles, and whether pressure leads to turnovers.[^3_1][^3_3]
- Transition moments, such as how quickly teams regain shape after losing the ball or exploit space after recovery.[^3_9][^3_6]
- Set-piece patterns, including kick-offs, corners, free kicks, throw-ins, and rest-defense positioning.[^3_2][^3_3]
- Space control, meaning which areas each team occupies, protects, or concedes over time.[^3_4][^3_6]


## Tactical insights

- Which team controlled territory and possession phases more effectively.[^3_6][^3_1]
- Whether attacks came through the middle, half-spaces, or wide channels.[^3_5][^3_10]
- Whether the team used direct play, short buildup, or mixed build-up patterns.[^3_9][^3_5]
- Which players or zones were repeatedly targeted in buildup, progression, and final-third entries.[^3_10][^3_5]
- How well the defensive structure stayed connected when the ball moved from one side to the other.[^3_1][^3_6]


## Physical insights

- Which players covered the most ground and who had the highest sprint load.[^3_8][^3_7]
- Whether certain players were overworked compared with the rest of the team.[^3_11][^3_8]
- How match intensity changed across periods, especially after goals, cards, or tactical shifts.[^3_8][^3_11]
- Whether a player’s movement profile suggests a winger, fullback, midfielder, or striker role even if names are hidden.[^3_4][^3_5]


## Event insights

- Sequence analysis of possession chains, including how attacks start, progress, and break down.[^3_5][^3_9]
- Common failure points, like interceptions, bad first touches, blocked passes, or unsuccessful duels.[^3_2][^3_1]
- High-value event zones, such as areas where shots, key passes, recoveries, or turnovers happen most often.[^3_10][^3_5]
- Momentum swings after goals, cards, or repeated ball recoveries.[^3_3][^3_1]


## Useful outputs

- Shot map and xG map.
- Passing network graph.
- Average position map.
- Heatmaps for players or zones.
- Team shape snapshots by phase of play.
- Sprint/distance/load leaderboard.
- Pressing and recovery maps.
- Possession sequence and transition summaries.[^3_5][^3_10]

Your sample event table already supports basic sequence analysis, because it shows event type, sub-type, start/end locations, and the players involved, which is enough to reconstruct possessions and tactical phases.[^3_2][^3_5]

# But then how would you visualize all of these ? Can you visualize all of those??

How many types of visualizations would be there?

Suppose you ask a question, what do I need to give you to explain your question? What kind of questions would you ask if I have these data?

Yes — you can visualize **almost all** of those insights, but not always as one chart per insight. In practice, a full single-match football analytics dashboard usually ends up with about **15 to 25 distinct visualization types**, because several insights share the same visual form, and some need multiple views to explain properly.[^4_1][^4_2][^4_3]

## Visualization types

Here are the main visualization types you can use with tracking + event data:[^4_2][^4_3][^4_1]

1. Heatmaps — player movement, team occupation, defensive action zones.[^4_4][^4_1]
2. Pass maps — all passes plotted from start to end coordinates.[^4_1]
3. Passing networks — average positions plus connections between players.[^4_2]
4. Shot maps — shot locations, outcomes, xG-sized markers.[^4_1][^4_2]
5. Event maps — tackles, interceptions, recoveries, fouls, clearances by location.[^4_2]
6. Average position maps — where each player typically operated.[^4_2]
7. Convex hull / team shape plots — team spread and compactness.[^4_3]
8. Line-height and compactness charts — back line, midfield line, team length/width over time.[^4_5][^4_3]
9. Distance and speed timelines — intensity, sprint load, acceleration over match time.[^4_6][^4_7]
10. Momentum charts — xT, danger, shots, entries, or pressure by minute.[^4_2]
11. Possession sequence flow charts — attack buildup and progression chains.[^4_8][^4_1]
12. Zone-control or pitch-control maps — who controlled which spaces.[^4_8][^4_5]
13. Pressure maps — where and when pressure happened.[^4_9][^4_3]
14. Defensive action density maps — pressing and recoveries in zones.[^4_2]
15. Ball trajectory maps — ball movement path during possessions or key actions.[^4_3]
16. Player trajectory trails — movement of one or more players over a sequence.[^4_6][^4_3]
17. Freeze frames — one tactical moment shown as a static pitch snapshot.[^4_3]
18. Animated replays — movements over time for possessions, presses, or goals.[^4_10][^4_3]
19. Bar charts — counts like passes, duels, recoveries, shots.[^4_11]
20. Line charts — trends over time like speed, distance, pressure, possession share.[^4_11]
21. Scatter plots — relationships such as speed vs distance, pass risk vs success.[^4_11]
22. Radar charts — player role/profile comparison, though less reliable for match storytelling.[^4_12]
23. Tables with color encoding — player stats enhanced with conditional formatting.[^4_13]
24. Dashboards — one integrated view combining several visual modules.[^4_13][^4_11]

## How many really matter

If your goal is a **serious single-match analysis pack**, I would group them like this:[^4_3][^4_2]


| Layer | Typical count |
| :-- | :-- |
| Core pitch maps | 8 to 10 types [^4_1][^4_2] |
| Time-series views | 3 to 5 types [^4_11][^4_2] |
| Tactical structure views | 3 to 5 types [^4_5][^4_3] |
| Physical-performance views | 3 to 4 types [^4_6][^4_7] |
| Interactive/animated views | 2 to 4 types [^4_10][^4_3] |

So a realistic answer is **around 20 visualization types**, with maybe 8 to 12 used in a compact report and 15 to 25 used in a full dashboard or analysis notebook.[^4_3][^4_2]

## What I need

If you ask me to explain a specific football question from your data, I usually need these inputs:[^4_1][^4_3]

- Tracking data for both teams and ball, with frame, time, x-y coordinates, and ideally velocity or enough frame rate to derive it.[^4_3]
- Event data with event type, subtype, timestamps or frames, team, player IDs, and start/end locations.[^4_1]
- Metadata: pitch dimensions, frame rate, playing direction by half, kickoff team, substitutions, cards, goals, and whether coordinates are normalized from 0 to 1 or in meters.[^4_1][^4_3]
- A mapping of anonymized player IDs to at least role labels if possible, such as CB, FB, CM, winger, striker, because roles make the explanation much better even if names remain hidden.[^4_2][^4_3]


## Questions I’d ask

If you have this data, these are the kinds of questions I would ask to analyze it properly:[^4_5][^4_1][^4_2]

- Which team are we focusing on, or do you want both compared?[^4_2]
- Do you want tactical analysis, player performance, physical output, or all three?[^4_7][^4_14]
- Are the x-y coordinates normalized or in real pitch meters?[^4_1]
- Which way was each team attacking in each half?[^4_3][^4_1]
- Do you have substitutions and role information, or only anonymous IDs?[^4_3]
- Do you want full-match analysis or only phases like buildup, final third, pressing, transitions, or set pieces?[^4_5][^4_2]
- Do you want static visuals, animations, or a dashboard?[^4_10][^4_13]

A few strong football questions you can answer from this data are:[^4_5][^4_2][^4_3]

- How did each team build up from the back?
- Where did each team progress the ball most often?
- Which player was the main hub in possession?
- Did the defending team press high or sit in a block?
- How compact was each team without the ball?
- Which turnovers created dangerous attacks?
- Which zones produced the best chances?
- Which anonymous players behaved like fullbacks, pivots, wingers, or strikers?
- How did team shape change after losing or regaining possession?
- Which possession sequences led to shots or entries into dangerous zones?[^4_1][^4_2]

# I meant what kind of football questions would you ask from these data?

Suppose yourself as playing two roles - one as an football analyst and one as a football coach.

You have a ipad on you, the coach asks you questions, you wizard-use your tool and output the result and show it to the coach. What will you show? just one visual, reports, or what??

On the iPad, I would not show “everything”; I’d show a **small set of visuals and clips that directly answer the coach’s specific question**, then have the ability to drill down if they want more.[^5_1][^5_2]

Below I’ll play both roles (coach vs analyst) and make the questions very concrete.

***

## Core idea: what the coach actually sees

Professional workflows use **simple live views plus deeper post-game reports**: a few key indicators on the bench, then richer dashboards and video playlists later.[^5_3][^5_2][^5_1]

- In-game: 1–3 visuals at a time (team shape, key stats, maybe a clip list), big and simple.[^5_4][^5_1]
- Post-game: a report/dashboard with 10–20 visuals plus linked video clips, used in meetings or 1‑to‑1 feedback.[^5_5][^5_6]

So on the iPad I’d think in **layers**:

1. Quick answer visual(s) for the coach’s question (1–2 screens).
2. Supporting numbers/tables underneath.
3. Tap to open precise video clips for context.

***

## Role 1: Coach questions about tactics

### 1. “Are we too open between the lines?”

**What I show:**

- **Freeze-frame average position map** for our team out of possession (one image): back line, midfield line, front line, with the ball position overlaid.[^5_7]
- **Team compactness timeline**: line chart of team length (distance between deepest defender and highest player) over time; highlight big spikes.[^5_8][^5_7]
- Linked **video clips** of phases where team length exceeds a threshold.

**Why:** Coach sees in seconds *where* the gaps are and *when* they appeared, then watches the clips to understand the “why”.[^5_7][^5_5]

***

### 2. “Where are they hurting us most with the ball?”

**What I show:**

- **Opposition pass map** with density (heatmap + arrows) showing repeated passing lanes and zones entered.[^5_9][^5_10]
- **Shot map** of the opposition (locations + xG-size markers).[^5_10][^5_9]
- Simple **zone bar chart**: shots / entries from left, central, right channels.

**Why:** This answers: “Left side or right side?”, “Central pocket or wide cross?” in one glance.[^5_9][^5_10]

***

### 3. “Is our press working?”

**What I show:**

- **Pressing heatmap**: locations of our defensive actions (tackles, challenges, interceptions, ball recoveries).[^5_10][^5_7]
- **Time-series of passes allowed per defensive action (PPDA-like)** per period.[^5_11][^5_3]
- Video playlist: every opposition build-up starting in their third and ending in our half, tagged as “pressed out” vs “played through”.

**Why:** Combine location (heatmap) + intensity (PPDA-line) + clips to see if the idea (high press or mid-block) is happening on the pitch.[^5_1][^5_11][^5_7]

***

### 4. “How are we building out from the back?”

**What I show:**

- **Pass network**: average positions of our players + pass connections (thicker edges = more passes).[^5_9][^5_10]
- **Buildup sequence flow**: sankey/flow diagram from zones (GK zone → defensive third → middle third → final third).[^5_12][^5_9]
- Short clip playlist of successful and failed build-ups (tagged by event chains).

**Why:** Coach sees instantly if we’re funneling through one side, overusing a CB, or skipping midfield.[^5_12][^5_10][^5_9]

***

### 5. “Who is free between the lines?”

**What I show:**

- **Freeze frame with pitch control overlay** at the moment of reception: zones colored by which team controls space.[^5_8][^5_12]
- **Scatter plot**: receptions between lines vs outcome (turn vs play forward / shot / foul won).

**Why:** Connect geometry (space control) with outcomes; ideal to decide on adjustments (e.g., move pivot, adjust 10’s positioning).[^5_7][^5_8][^5_12]

***

### 6. “Are we defending crosses well?”

**What I show:**

- **Cross map**: opposition cross start and end locations, outcome color-coded (clearance, shot, goal, headed away).[^5_10][^5_9]
- Short clip playlist of conceded crosses by flank.

**Why:** No need for big tables; coach sees patterns like “all dangerous crosses are from our right flank to back post”.[^5_9][^5_10]

***

## Role 2: Coach questions about individuals

### 7. “Is Player X doing what we asked in possession?”

**What I show:**

- **Player heatmap** (in possession phases): their touches and movement zones.[^5_13][^5_9]
- **Pass map for that player**: passes attempted, directions, lengths.[^5_10][^5_9]
- A small **box of KPIs**: passes completed, progressive passes, turnovers, etc.

Then:

- 4–6 **video clips**: good behaviors vs breakdowns, annotated.

**Why:** This supports a 1‑to‑1 conversation later, not just an in-game adjustment.[^5_5][^5_3]

***

### 8. “Is Player Y running enough / too much?”

**What I show:**

- **Distance and high-intensity running bar chart** vs team average and that player’s typical baseline.[^5_14][^5_15]
- **Speed/time chart** with segments colored by intensity zone.[^5_16][^5_4]

**Why:** For substitutions and load management, the coach wants simple comparisons: “above, at, or below expected?”.[^5_4][^5_14][^5_16]

***

### 9. “How is our new shape after the substitution?”

**What I show:**

- Before/after **average position maps** for the team (2 panels, same scale).[^5_7][^5_10]
- Short **shifted-shape freeze frames** at a key phase (e.g., out of possession mid-block).

**Why:** This makes structural change visible, not just a gut feeling.[^5_1][^5_7]

***

## Role 3: Analyst’s own questions (things I’d proactively check)

Even if the coach doesn’t ask, I’d quietly monitor certain things and be ready when they ask:[^5_17][^5_6][^5_7]

- Are transition moments (both directions) under control?
- Are we repeatedly losing the ball in specific zones?
- Is there an obvious spacing issue (too flat front line, CBs too wide/too narrow)?
- Are our set pieces (for and against) producing the patterns we trained?

For each, I’d have a **small template view**:

- **Transition view:** sequences starting from loss/recovery, showing time to shot/entry and basic map.
- **Turnover zones:** event map for ball lost, colored by outcome (immediate shot conceded or not).
- **Set-piece maps:** corners and free kicks (start and first contact locations).

***

## What format: one visual vs full report?

### In-match (bench / iPad):

- Show **one main visual per specific question**, plus one supporting chart or number block.
- Attach 3–10 **tagged clips** the coach can open instantly.[^5_2][^5_1]
- Keep it **big, minimal and readable**: no tiny fonts or overloaded dashboards.

Think “**view per question**”, not “all dashboards at once”.

### Post-match (meeting or review):

- Build a **structured report** organized by coach’s priority:
    - Team phases: build-up, chance creation, defending, transitions, set pieces.
    - Individuals: per player 1–2 pages each.
- Each section: 2–3 visuals + 1–2 KPI tables + links to video playlists.[^5_6][^5_3][^5_5]

***

## Concrete example: one Q\&A flow

Coach: “Why are they getting so much joy down our left?”

I’d answer with:

1. **Visual 1:** Opposition attack map (entries and crosses) filtered to our left side; obvious red blob on our left flank.
2. **Visual 2:** Freeze frame showing our back line and fullback positioning in one conceded cross.
3. **Support:** Clip playlist of those attacks, with drawing tools showing distances and lines.[^5_5][^5_1]

And I’d say: “Here are all their dangerous attacks; 80% are entering this zone behind our left-back; we can either drop the winger deeper, push the 6 wider, or adjust our line height.”

***

Given those constraints, I’d treat this as a **post‑match, single‑game, no‑video, coach–analyst debrief**. The flow is: pre‑process the anonymized data into something coach‑readable, then run a structured conversation where the coach asks tactical questions and I answer with a **small number of focused visuals per question**, not a huge dump.[^6_1][^6_2][^6_3]

I’ll structure this in three parts:

1. What I prepare **before** sitting with the coach.
2. How the **conversation** plays out (questions the coach asks, what I show).
3. How anonymization and lack of video change what I do.

***

## 1. Pre‑processing before meeting the coach

Even with anonymous IDs, I’d build a “pre-layer” so the coach sees something intuitive rather than “Player11 / Player25”.[^6_4][^6_1]

### 1.1. Reconstruct lineups and roles

From tracking data: positions over time, frame-by-frame.

- Infer **starting XI and substitutions** by continuous presence in tracking data.
- Assign **roles** by average position heatmap and movement patterns, e.g.:
    - “Home–CB1”, “Home–LB”, “Home–CM2”, “Home–RW”, “Home–9”.
    - Same for away team: “Away–RB”, “Away–pivot”, etc.
- Keep the true IDs in the backend but show role labels on visuals.

This is feasible and in line with typical tracking-data visual analytics systems that infer roles from positioning.[^6_2][^6_3][^6_1]

### 1.2. Basic match context pack

I’d build a compact “context page” that we both know before questions start:[^6_5][^6_6]

- Formation and average positions for both teams.
- Simple match stats:
    - shots, xG (if we infer it), possession %, passes, PPDA-type metric.
- Time‑line of goals/cards and major events.

This gives a shared mental map; we both watched the game, so this is just anchoring.[^6_7][^6_5]

## 2. How the conversation plays out

Now imagine we’re in a meeting room, both remembering the game. Coach asks; I answer. I’ll show **at most 2–3 visuals per topic**, all derived from that single-match tracking+event dataset.[^6_1][^6_2]

### 2.1. Topic A – In‑possession structure

**Coach:** “We felt our build-up was too slow / too predictable. How did we actually build?”

**Analyst:**

**Visuals I show:**

1. **Our pass network** (full match or by phase).
    - Nodes: average positions of our players.
    - Edges: number of passes; thickness shows volume.
    - Color: maybe final‑third involvement.
This is exactly the kind of multi-player view in typical soccer visual analytics systems.[^6_3][^6_5][^6_1]
2. **Zone-to-zone progression map**
    - Sankey or heat of passes from:
        - defensive third → midfield third → final third.
    - Show where chains break: e.g. we repeatedly lose it in left half-space.
3. Simple **table/summary**, e.g.:
    - % of passes forward vs backward,
    - number of passes into final third,
    - number of entries into box.

**Conversation outcome:**
We can say things like “We built mainly via our left CB → LB → LW chain” or “The pivot rarely received between lines”, backed by one main visual and a couple of stats.[^6_6][^6_5]

***

### 2.2. Topic B – Out‑of‑possession / block \& press

**Coach:** “Were we really in a compact block, or did we stretch too much when they switched play?”

**Analyst:**

**Visuals I show:**

1. **Average out‑of‑possession shape**
    - Two freeze-frame style plots:
        - Our team’s average positions when ball in our half.
        - When ball in their half.
    - Shows block height and width.
2. **Compactness metrics over time**
    - Line charts:
        - team length (front-to-back distance),
        - team width,
        - average distance between lines.
These metrics and their visualisations are standard for analyzing collective dynamics.[^6_8][^6_2][^6_1]
3. **Defensive action map**
    - Tackles, interceptions, ball recoveries plotted by location.
    - We see if pressure is high, mid, or low.

**Conversation outcome:**
We translate the coach’s “we looked stretched” into: “In minutes 30–45 our team length spiked; their switches of play exploited our left-side gap”, all derived from tracking+event data.[^6_8][^6_1]

***

### 2.3. Topic C – Transition phases

**Coach:** “We spoke about counter-press; did we actually recover the ball quickly after losing it?”

**Analyst:**

**Visuals I show:**

1. **Transition sequences view**
    - For each ball loss, track:
        - time until we recover,
        - distance the ball traveled,
        - whether they entered our final third.
    - Show distribution as bar/box plot or scatter.
2. **Loss location map**
    - Where we lost the ball (events: BALL LOST / CHALLENGE GROUND-LOST / etc.).
    - Overlaid with whether that led to a shot against within, say, 10 seconds.

These kinds of event-based views are specifically highlighted in tools that combine event and tracking data for post-game analysis.[^6_4][^6_5]

**Conversation outcome:**
We can say: “Our counter-press worked in central zones but not on the right wing; 3 of their dangerous counters started from right‑side turnovers” – again, coach‑meaningful language on top of a simple visual.

***

### 2.4. Topic D – Chance creation \& finishing

**Coach:** “We had pressure but maybe not dangerous chances. What did our chances look like?”

**Analyst:**

**Visuals I show:**

1. **Our shot map**
    - Shot locations, with markers sized by xG estimate (if we can compute) and color by outcome.
    - Single visual, full match, like a “Finishing Map”.[^6_5]
2. **Key pass map**
    - Passes that directly create shots (event type PASS → SHOT chains).
    - Shows where key passes originate and where they go.[^6_5]
3. Short **numeric summary**
    - xG total vs opponent (if computed),
    - number of shots inside vs outside box,
    - number of big chances.

The Driblab type “post game analysis” uses exactly this flow: finishing map → key pass map → game flow, to structure coach discussions.[^6_5]

**Conversation outcome:**
We can confirm or challenge the feeling “we created enough”: maybe volume was high but quality low, or vice versa.

***

### 2.5. Topic E – Individual roles \& performance

**Coach:** “Our ‘pivot’ (say Home–CM1) – did he give us the right support?”

**Analyst:**

**Visuals I show (for that role):**

1. **Positional heatmap**
    - Only tracking periods when we have the ball.
    - Show where he actually played: anchor, too high, too wide, etc.
Heatmaps for player performance are a standard way to visualise movement and area usage.[^6_9][^6_1]
2. **Involvement map**
    - All passes received, with arrows for passes played.
    - Summarise:
        - receptions between lines,
        - switches, progressive passes.
3. Simple **stat box**
    - number of passes,
    - forward vs backward proportion,
    - turnovers in dangerous zones.

**Conversation outcome:**
Coach gets: “He stayed too flat with the CBs / He rarely occupied the half-space,” grounded in a single match’s tracking and event data.

## 3. How anonymization and no video change the process

### 3.1. Dealing with anonymization

You were right about the assumption: we need **one more layer** between raw data and coach:[^6_1][^6_4]

- We never show “Player11”; we show “Home–LB” or “Away–9”.
- Internally, we may keep a crosswalk table:
    - `Player11 → Home–LB`,
    - `Player1 → Home–GK`, etc.
- Coach and analyst both watched the game, so they mentally know “Home–LB” is “our usual left-back”; anonymization doesn’t hurt explanation much.

This is standard practice when analytical tools combine tracking and event data but abstract away direct identities for specific use cases.[^6_4]

### 3.2. No video: lean on geometry \& sequences

Without video, we can’t show clips; we must rely purely on:

- Geometry over time (tracking).
- Event sequences (event log).
That’s exactly what visual analytics systems like those described by Janetzko et al. and Santos et al. do: single-player views, multi-player views, and event-based views, all on top of the same match.[^6_2][^6_3][^6_1]

But since both coach and analyst *remember* the situations, a simple visual (“this cross came from here to there”) is enough to re-trigger their mental video.

***

## 4. Overall answer: what I actually “play out” with the coach

Putting it together, the post‑match session looks like this:

1. **We start with a one-page context view**
    - Formations, average positions, basic stats.
    - We agree on what we saw.
2. **Coach picks a theme**
    - e.g., build-up, pressing, transitions, set pieces, a specific player.
3. **I answer each theme with**
    - 1 main visual (map/network/timeline),
    - 1 supporting visual or concise table,
    - 1–2 spoken sentences linking back to what we both saw live.
4. **We log conclusions**
    - e.g., “Press shape ok until 60’, then compactness drops” or
    - “Right flank transitions vulnerable”.
5. **We then translate into training actions**, but that’s beyond the data session itself – coaches’ domain.

So: I don’t try to “visualize everything”. I pre‑compute a small library of views (say 15–20, as we discussed earlier) and then, in the meeting, act as a **search engine over that library**, pulling the right 2–3 visuals per coach question and narrating them in football language.[^6_10][^6_7][^6_1]

Yes — that is the right way to think about it. The coach usually goes **level by level**: first identify who was on the pitch and in what structure, then ask phase-specific questions, then zoom into more advanced tactical patterns.[^7_1][^7_2]

So the best design is a **progressive analysis flow**:

1. orientation,
2. intermediate diagnosis,
3. advanced investigation.[^7_3][^7_1]

## Level 1 setup

If the coach starts with “show me my players, substitute times, lineups,” I would show a clean **match orientation page** with 4 things only: lineup, role labels, substitution timeline, and average positions/likely formation.[^7_1][^7_3]

### What to show first

- **Lineup table**: Home and Away, player IDs relabeled into role-like names such as Home-CB1, Home-LB, Home-CM1, with start and end minute on pitch.[^7_1]
- **Substitution timeline**: a horizontal minute bar showing when each role entered or exited.[^7_2]
- **Average position map**: one pitch plot per team showing mean locations of players while in possession and optionally out of possession.[^7_3][^7_1]
- **Likely formation label**: e.g. “approx. 4-3-3” or “4-2-3-1 out of possession,” inferred from average positions and spacing, but shown as an estimate rather than absolute truth.[^7_3]

That page answers the coach’s basic grounding question: “Who was playing, where, and for how long?”[^7_1][^7_2]

## Level 2 questions

Then the coach usually moves into **functional questions**: how were we passing, where were we building, who connected with whom, where did we lose control, and what changed by phase or minute window.[^7_1][^7_4]

### Good intermediate questions

- Show me our pass network in the first half.[^7_4]
- Show me the pass network from 30 to 35 minutes.[^7_5]
- Show me the defender-to-winger connection on the left side.[^7_4]
- Show me where we progressed the ball most often.[^7_6]
- Show me where we lost the ball.[^7_7]
- Show me our average shape when defending in our own half.[^7_3]
- Show me their attacks that entered our box.[^7_7]


### What to show for these

For a question like “show me the pass network around 30–35 mins of the defender with the winger,” I would show:[^7_4][^7_5]

1. **Time-window pass network** for 30:00–35:00.
2. **Filtered pair connection map** just for defender ↔ winger combinations.
3. **Event list panel** beside it with passes, turnovers, recoveries in that window.

That is a valid and actually **good** coach question, because passing networks are often used to study which players are central, which connections dominate buildup, and whether certain channels are overused or blocked.[^7_5][^7_4]

## Level 3 questions

Advanced questions are usually not “show me a pretty chart,” but “prove or disprove a tactical feeling”. The coach may not phrase them perfectly, but the analyst should translate them into data questions.[^7_1][^7_3][^7_8]

### Typical advanced coach questions

- Were we too stretched between defense and midfield?[^7_3][^7_9]
- Did our press actually force them wide?[^7_3]
- Which player was the real buildup hub, not just the one with most passes?[^7_4][^7_5]
- Did our shape change after the substitution?[^7_1]
- Were we vulnerable immediately after losing the ball?[^7_7]
- Did they overload one flank?[^7_6]
- Which zones produced their best chances?[^7_7]
- Were our fullbacks too high at the wrong moments?[^7_3]


### What to show for these

For advanced questions, I would usually show **2 linked visuals**, not one:[^7_1][^7_2]

- A **structure visual**, such as shape map, compactness timeline, or pitch occupation map.[^7_3][^7_9]
- An **event consequence visual**, such as shot map, turnover map, or dangerous-entry map.[^7_6][^7_7]

Example: if the coach asks “Were we too stretched?”, I would show:

- team length / width over time, and
- freeze-frame average shape or pitch occupation in the problem phases.[^7_9][^7_3]


## Best hierarchy

A strong single-match coach-facing workflow would look like this:[^7_1][^7_2]


| Level | Coach question type | Main output |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| 1. Orientation | Who played, where, when? | Lineup table, subs timeline, average positions [^7_1][^7_2] |
| 2. Functional | How did we pass/build/defend in a phase? | Time-window pass network, event map, phase stats [^7_4][^7_6] |
| 3. Tactical | Why did this pattern happen? | Linked structure + consequence visuals [^7_3][^7_7] |
| 4. Decision | What changed, what must we fix? | Before/after comparison views, concise report notes [^7_1][^7_8] |

## What the analyst should do

The analyst’s job is to convert vague coach language into a visual answer path. So if the coach says something messy like “show me that time our defender kept forcing it wide to the winger,” the analyst should translate it into:[^7_1][^7_8]

- pick the minute window,
- filter to passes involving that defender and winger,
- show the pass network and pitch map,
- compare it with adjacent windows if needed.[^7_4][^7_5]

So the coach does **not** need to know the exact technical query; the analyst interprets the football question and chooses the right visual.[^7_8][^7_1]

## Practical rule

Use **one page per question level**, and within that page show only 1 main visual plus 1–2 supporting views. Too many charts at once weakens the conversation; the best systems use overview-plus-detail, where the coach starts broad and drills down only when something looks important.[^7_1][^7_2][^7_3]

A very practical page order would be:

1. Match setup.
2. Team shape and lineups.
3. Passing and buildup.
4. Defensive structure.
5. Transitions and ball losses.
6. Chances and shots.
7. Player-specific views.
8. Before/after phase comparisons.[^7_3][^7_7][^7_1]

**coach question → analyst interpretation → exact visualization to show → what insight it answers**.

# Now, suppose, we replace the analyst - and instead make the tablet intelligent. Then how does it play out?

Basically now, the coach asks the tablet - through voice, the tablet processes and understands the proper requirement of the coach and then produces the intended results.

Do you understand? How does it all play out now?? How would the dashboard work now?

Yes — now the tablet becomes a **conversational visual analytics system**: the coach asks in football language, the system translates that into data operations, chooses the right view, and returns a small answer panel with drill-down options instead of waiting for a human analyst to interpret the question.[^8_1][^8_2][^8_3]

So it is no longer just a dashboard. It becomes a **question-driven dashboard** with voice input, intent detection, auto-filtering, and progressive visual responses.[^8_4][^8_5]

## How it plays out

The coach speaks naturally, for example: “Show me our lineup,” “What changed after the sub?”, or “Were we too open on the left between 30 and 45?”.[^8_3][^8_5]
The tablet then does four things in sequence: understand the request, map it to football entities and time windows, retrieve the relevant match slices, and render the most useful visual plus a short explanation.[^8_2][^8_4]

A typical flow would be:

1. Coach asks by voice.
2. Tablet transcribes and detects intent, such as lineup, passing, shape, transitions, or player role.[^8_5][^8_3]
3. Tablet asks a clarifying question only if needed, for example “Do you mean our team or the opposition?”.[^8_4]
4. Tablet shows one primary visual, one support view, and a one- or two-sentence answer.[^8_1][^8_5]
5. Coach follows up naturally, for example “Now only show first half” or “Compare before and after minute 60”.[^8_3]

## Dashboard structure

The dashboard should be built around **three layers**: orientation, exploration, and diagnosis.[^8_6][^8_2]

### 1. Orientation layer

This is the default home state before any question is asked. It should show:[^8_6][^8_2]

- Team lineups with anonymized role labels.
- Substitution timeline.
- Average positions and likely formation.
- Match timeline with major events.

This gives the coach a starting mental map of the match.[^8_2]

### 2. Exploration layer

When the coach asks a mid-level question, the tablet opens a contextual analysis card rather than a brand-new page.[^8_5][^8_4]

For example:

- “Show me our pass network from 30 to 35 minutes.”
- “Show only connections involving the left-back and left winger.”
- “Where did we lose the ball most in the second half?”[^8_7][^8_8]

The tablet responds with:

- one main pitch-based visual,
- one supporting trend/table,
- chips for follow-up actions like **Compare**, **Expand window**, **Filter player**, **Show consequence**.[^8_4][^8_3]


### 3. Diagnosis layer

This is for advanced tactical questions where the coach is really asking for explanation, not just display.[^8_6][^8_2]

For example:

- “Were we too stretched?”
- “Did our press force them wide?”
- “Did the substitution improve right-side progression?”[^8_9][^8_6]

The tablet should answer with linked views:

- a structure view, such as shape/compactness/occupation,
- an outcome view, such as turnovers, entries, or shots,
- a compact written explanation.[^8_2][^8_6]


## What the system needs internally

To behave intelligently, the tablet needs an internal pipeline, not just charts.[^8_3][^8_2]

### Input understanding

It must identify:

- team reference: us / them / home / away,
- time reference: full match / first half / 30–35 minutes / after sub,
- object reference: player, unit, flank, formation, transition,
- task type: show, compare, explain, summarize.[^8_5][^8_3]


### Football translation layer

This is the key replacement for the analyst. It maps coach language to data logic.[^8_6][^8_2]

Examples:

- “Show my players” → lineup table + role inference + substitution times.
- “Show defender to winger network” → filtered pass graph for selected roles and time window.
- “Were we open?” → compactness metrics + average out-of-possession shape.
- “Did we create enough?” → shot map + key pass map + box entries.[^8_8][^8_7][^8_6]


### Visual selection engine

The system should not always use the same chart. It should choose the best visual by question type.[^8_1][^8_4]

Examples:

- Identity/setup questions → tables + average position maps.
- Connection questions → pass networks.
- Space questions → heatmaps or occupation maps.
- Timing questions → timelines or minute-by-minute trend charts.
- Causality-style tactical questions → paired visuals.[^8_2][^8_6]


## Example conversation

Here’s how it might feel in practice:

**Coach:** “Show me my players and subs.”
**Tablet:** Shows lineup table, average positions, likely formation, and substitution bars; says “Your team started in an approximate 4-3-3, with two role changes after minute 62”.[^8_6][^8_2]

**Coach:** “Now show me our pass network between 30 and 35 minutes.”
**Tablet:** Shows time-filtered pass network and event list; offers buttons: *Compare 25–30*, *Only left side*, *Show turnovers after passes*.[^8_10][^8_7]

**Coach:** “Only show left-back to left-winger.”
**Tablet:** Filters the graph to that pair and overlays pass directions on the pitch.[^8_7]

**Coach:** “Did that actually help us progress?”
**Tablet:** Switches to paired view: left-side pass map plus final-third entry count before and after those combinations.[^8_11][^8_8]

**Coach:** “Were we too open after losing the ball?”
**Tablet:** Shows turnover map plus compactness timeline in the 10 seconds after losses.[^8_12][^8_6]

That is the core experience: each answer becomes the next question’s context.[^8_3][^8_5]

## Screen design

A smart tablet dashboard for this should have five persistent UI regions:[^8_4][^8_5]


| Area | Purpose |
| :-- | :-- |
| Voice/query bar | Coach speaks or types the question [^8_3] |
| Main canvas | Primary visual answer, usually a pitch map or network [^8_2] |
| Support panel | Secondary chart, summary stats, or filters [^8_4] |
| Match context bar | Current team, half, minute range, players, phase [^8_5] |
| Follow-up suggestions | Auto-generated next questions like “Compare before/after sub” [^8_3] |

This matters because the coach should not have to “navigate dashboards” manually. The interface should preserve context and keep the conversation flowing.[^8_4][^8_3]

## Best interaction model

The best model is **overview first, answer second, drill down third**.[^8_1][^8_2]

So the tablet should:

- start from a stable match overview,
- answer one coach question with one focused view,
- keep the current filters visible,
- allow natural follow-ups without resetting the screen.[^8_2][^8_4]

It should also suggest next-step prompts such as:

- “Show the same for the opposition.”
- “Compare before and after the substitution.”
- “Explain why this network changed.”
- “Show the related turnovers and shots.”[^8_5][^8_3]


## What makes it intelligent

What replaces the analyst is not just speech-to-text. It is the system’s ability to do **intent resolution + football-aware query mapping + visual choice + follow-up guidance**.[^8_3][^8_2]

So intelligence here means:

- understanding vague coach language,
- translating it into data slices,
- selecting visuals automatically,
- summarizing what changed,
- proposing the next useful question.[^8_1][^8_5]

In short, the tablet becomes a **football conversation partner** built on top of a visual analytics engine, not just a chart library.[^8_6][^8_2]

A strong next step would be to design the actual product as:

1. **system architecture** for voice-to-visual pipeline, or
2. **screen-by-screen dashboard UX** for the tablet.

# Suppose now, let's go now more step and make it an assistant - fully commandable by voice and interactive with screen transitions based on the questions asked by the coach.

Yes — now it becomes a **multimodal football copilot**: the coach speaks, the assistant listens, understands the football intent, updates the screen with smooth transitions, speaks back briefly, and keeps the whole interaction grounded in the current match context.[^9_1][^9_2][^9_3]

This is no longer a static dashboard with filters. It is a **voice-first, screen-supported operating system for match analysis** where conversation drives navigation, analysis, and comparison.[^9_4][^9_5]

## How it plays out

The coach should be able to talk naturally, not memorize commands, because strong voice interfaces are built around utterance → intent → slots → prompt, with clarification only when required.[^9_6][^9_1]
So the interaction loop becomes:

1. Coach speaks a question.
2. Assistant transcribes and identifies intent.
3. Assistant fills missing slots like team, time window, player group, phase, or comparison target.[^9_7][^9_6]
4. Screen transitions to the correct visual state.
5. Assistant gives a short spoken answer and suggests useful follow-ups.[^9_3][^9_1]

Example:

- “Show me our lineup.”
- “Now only first half.”
- “Focus on the left side.”
- “Compare before and after the sub.”
- “Why did that change?”[^9_4][^9_3]

Each request should preserve context from the last one, because conversational assistants work best when they are context-aware rather than reactive from scratch every turn.[^9_8][^9_3]

## Interface model

A good interface here is **voice first, visual second, touch optional**.[^9_9][^9_1]
That means the coach can complete the core task by speaking alone, while the screen gives confirmation, visual explanation, and drill-down control.[^9_7][^9_1]

### Screen zones

The tablet should have five persistent regions:[^9_2][^9_3]

- **Listening bar** at the bottom or top, showing mic status, transcript, and current understanding.
- **Main visual canvas** in the center, where pitch maps, pass networks, timelines, and comparisons appear.
- **Context ribbon** showing current filters: team, half, minute range, players, phase.
- **Insight panel** with one-sentence explanation and key stats.
- **Suggested prompts** like “Compare first vs second half” or “Show only turnovers.”[^9_5][^9_3]

The key point is that the assistant changes the screen **for** the coach instead of making the coach operate menus.[^9_5][^9_4]

## Conversation design

The assistant needs a strong football-specific conversation layer. Voice UX guidance recommends designing happy paths, clarification paths, and repair paths rather than only one-shot commands.[^9_1][^9_6]

### Intent types

Your football assistant would need intents such as:[^9_6][^9_7]

- Show setup: lineup, substitutions, formation, average positions.
- Filter view: first half, second half, 30–35 mins, after minute 60.
- Focus entity: team, flank, unit, player role, player pair.
- Ask explanation: why, where, who, when, what changed.
- Compare states: before vs after, us vs them, player A vs player B.[^9_3]


### Slots

Each voice request should extract slots such as:[^9_6]

- Team: us, them, home, away.
- Time: full match, first half, 30–35, after substitution.
- Object: lineup, press, winger, left-back, shape, transitions.
- Operation: show, compare, explain, summarize.
- Scope: full team, unit, player pair, zone.

Example:
“Show me the left-back to winger connection from 30 to 35”
becomes:

- intent = show passing relationship
- team = current selected team
- players = left-back, left-winger
- time = 30:00–35:00[^9_3][^9_6]


## Screen transitions

The transitions matter a lot. Voice UI guidance specifically recommends avoiding hard cuts and keeping voice and visuals synchronized so the system feels alive, not laggy.[^9_10][^9_1]

### Transition rules

- **Listening state**: mic pulse, live transcript, dimmed but stable background.[^9_10][^9_1]
- **Understanding state**: short animation on recognized entities like “left side,” “30–35,” “our team.”
- **Loading state**: visual placeholders on the next screen, not blank waiting.[^9_2][^9_7]
- **Result state**: canvas morphs into the new chart instead of replacing the whole page abruptly.[^9_1]
- **Drill-down state**: when coach says “go deeper,” side panels expand and context ribbon updates without resetting the main view.[^9_9][^9_3]

A line chart can slide in from a timeline interaction, a pitch map can zoom into the left half-space, and a pass network can fade-filter down to two players. That makes the assistant feel like it is **following the question** rather than jumping randomly.[^9_10][^9_1]

## Example flow

Here is a realistic interaction:

**Coach:** “Show me my players and substitutions.”
**Assistant:** “Here is your starting group, estimated roles, and substitution times.” The screen transitions from overview to lineup cards, sub timeline, and average positions.[^9_11][^9_2]

**Coach:** “Now show me the left side from 30 to 35 minutes.”
**Assistant:** “Focusing on your left channel between 30 and 35 minutes.” The context ribbon updates; the pitch zooms to left-side actions and the time slider snaps to that window.[^9_1][^9_3]

**Coach:** “Show me the fullback and winger connection.”
**Assistant:** “Displaying passes and receptions between those two roles.” The pass network filters to just that edge, with a support panel showing count, success rate, and progression.[^9_12][^9_2]

**Coach:** “Did it actually help us progress?”
**Assistant:** “Yes, this connection produced three final-third entries in that window, but no box entries.” The screen transitions to a paired view: filtered pass map on the left, progression outcome summary on the right.[^9_13][^9_4]

**Coach:** “Compare that with after the substitution.”
**Assistant:** “Comparing before and after the substitution.” The view becomes a side-by-side split state with identical scales.[^9_5][^9_3]

That is the ideal behavior: each spoken query becomes a new visual state built from the previous one.[^9_2][^9_3]

## Dashboard behavior

A normal dashboard expects the user to browse. Your assistant dashboard should instead behave like a **state machine** driven by conversation.[^9_8][^9_2]

### State layers

- **Home state**: lineup, average positions, match timeline.
- **Focus state**: one selected theme such as passing, shape, chances, transitions.
- **Filtered state**: minute range, half, side, player pair, phase.
- **Comparison state**: before/after, us/them, first/second half.
- **Explanation state**: auto-summary plus evidence visuals.[^9_2][^9_3]

The coach should always be able to say:

- “Go back.”
- “Reset.”
- “Only show our team.”
- “Compare to the first half.”
- “Why?”
- “Explain that.”[^9_14][^9_1]


## Response style

The assistant’s spoken response should be short, because voice systems should limit information to what users can remember easily.[^9_14][^9_1]
So the tablet should speak in this format:

- Acknowledge the request.
- State the action taken.
- Give one concise finding.
- Offer 1–2 relevant next moves.[^9_6][^9_3]

Example:
“I’ve filtered to your left side from 30 to 35 minutes. The fullback-to-winger link was active, but it produced no box entries. You can compare this with the second half or see the surrounding turnovers.”[^9_14][^9_3]

## Error handling

The assistant must handle ambiguity gracefully, because good VUI systems minimize dead-end “I don’t understand” responses and instead repair the conversation.[^9_7][^9_1]

Examples:

- “Show me him” → “Do you mean the left-back or the winger?”[^9_1]
- “Show me after the sub” → “Do you mean the substitution at minute 62 or 74?”[^9_6]
- “Show me our press” → “Would you like defensive actions, team compactness, or pass pressure?”[^9_7]

That repair behavior is what makes it feel like an **assistant**, not just voice search.[^9_3][^9_1]

## What you are really building

From a product perspective, this is a combination of:[^9_5][^9_2]

- a football ontology, meaning roles, phases, zones, events, match states;
- a conversational layer, meaning intents, slots, clarifications, memory;
- a visual analytics engine, meaning chart selection, filtering, and comparisons;
- a multimodal interface, meaning voice + visuals + touch working together cleanly.[^9_9][^9_3]

So the final product is best thought of as a **voice-commanded tactical intelligence tablet**, not a dashboard with a microphone attached.[^9_4][^9_5]

A strong next design step is to define:

1. the **end-to-end conversation flow**, or
2. the **screen-by-screen product UX**, including transitions and persistent UI regions.
