# But then how would you calculate or represent all the questions of the coach and analyst?

I mean we just have numbers and coordinates right? How would you quantify or measure those questions - the answer to those questions - be it analyst or coach questions??

Hope you are understanding what I'm trying to say.

Yes — I understand exactly what you mean.

You’re asking: **how do vague football questions become measurable answers if the raw input is only coordinates, timestamps, and event labels?**
The short answer is: you create a layer of **derived metrics, rules, and models** on top of the raw tracking/event data, and those become the measurable representations of coach and analyst questions.[^1][^2][^3]

So the pipeline is basically:

**raw coordinates/events → derived features → tactical metrics → visual explanations → answer to football question**[^4][^1]

## Core idea

A coach’s question is usually not directly measurable as written.
You first translate it into one or more measurable proxies, such as team width, team length, spacing, pass frequency, final-third entries, recoveries, xG, PPDA, or rest-defense structure.[^5][^6][^4]

Example:

- Coach asks: “Were we too open after losing the ball?”
- System translates that into:
    - team length after ball loss,
    - team width after ball loss,
    - distance between defensive and midfield lines,
    - number of players behind the ball,
    - time to defensive recovery,
    - shots or entries conceded within $n$ seconds[^5][^4][^1]

So football questions are answered through **operational definitions**.

## How to think about it

Every question needs 4 things:[^2][^3]


| Layer | Meaning |
| :-- | :-- |
| Football question | What the coach/analyst asks |
| Tactical interpretation | What that question means in football terms |
| Quantifiable metrics | What numbers can represent it |
| Visual output | How to show it clearly |

That is the bridge from “football language” to “data language”.[^3][^2]

## Example mappings

Here are the most important examples.

### 1. “Show me our shape”

This sounds visual, but it is still measurable.[^4][^1]

**Possible quantification**

- Average x-y position of each player.
- Team centroid.
- Team width: max x-distance or lateral spread.
- Team length: deepest-to-highest player distance.
- Convex hull area.
- Distance between units: defense–midfield, midfield–attack.[^1][^4]

**Representation**

- Average position map.
- Convex hull / shape overlay.
- Width and length summary cards.

***

### 2. “Were we compact enough?”

This is a classic coach question.[^4][^1]

**Possible quantification**

- Team length.
- Team width.
- Average nearest-neighbor distance.
- Distance between lines.
- Area occupied by outfield players.
- Variation in compactness by phase or minute window.[^1][^4]

**Representation**

- Compactness timeline.
- Frame-by-frame shape snapshots.
- Out-of-possession average structure.

***

### 3. “How did we build up?”

This must be turned into progression metrics.[^7][^2]

**Possible quantification**

- Number of passes in buildup phase.
- Average possession length.
- First-line exits from defensive third.
- Pass progression distance.
- Central vs wide progression share.
- Network centrality of buildup players.
- Frequency of pass patterns or pass archetypes.[^8][^7][^2]

**Representation**

- Pass network.
- Zone-to-zone progression map.
- Sequence flow chart.
- Directional pass map.

***

### 4. “Did our press work?”

This becomes pressure and disruption measurement.[^9][^6]

**Possible quantification**

- PPDA: passes allowed before a defensive action.
- Defensive actions in high/mid/low zones.
- Opponent forced wide rate.
- Opponent backward pass rate under pressure.
- Recoveries in advanced zones.
- Time from opponent receipt to defensive engagement.
- More advanced: xPPDA-like contextual pressure models.[^6][^9]

**Representation**

- Pressure heatmap.
- PPDA timeline.
- Opponent pass disruption maps.

***

### 5. “What happened when we lost the ball in attack?”

This is a transition question, which you were asking about earlier.[^5][^1]

**Possible quantification**

- Ball-loss location.
- Number of players behind the ball at loss.
- Rest-defense shape.
- Time to first defensive action.
- Time to regain compactness.
- Opponent progression distance within 5–10 seconds.
- Shots/box entries/xThreat conceded after those losses.[^5][^1]

**Representation**

- Turnover map.
- Transition replay window.
- Rest-defense snapshot.
- Consequence chart: harmless vs dangerous losses.

***

### 6. “Did we create enough danger?”

That becomes chance-quality measurement.[^10][^6]

**Possible quantification**

- Shots.
- Shot locations.
- xG.
- Big chances.
- Box entries.
- Key passes.
- xA or pass-to-shot value proxies.
- Sequence value before shots.[^6][^10]

**Representation**

- Shot map.
- Key pass map.
- Chance creation timeline.
- Sequence-to-shot breakdown.

***

### 7. “Who was our key player?”

This must be defined carefully.[^11][^8]

**Possible quantification**
Depending on meaning:

- Most touches.
- Most progressive passes.
- Highest network centrality.
- Most receptions between lines.
- Highest xA contribution.
- Most recoveries.
- Highest involvement in valuable sequences.
- Composite player impact metric.[^7][^8][^11]

**Representation**

- Role involvement dashboard.
- Network centrality chart.
- Player action map.


## The actual math layer

From just coordinates and times, you can derive a lot.[^4][^1]

### Directly computable from tracking

- Position $(x,y)$
- Velocity
- Acceleration
- Distance covered
- Sprint count
- Team centroid
- Spread / area
- Pairwise distances
- Distances between units
- Ball-player distance
- Orientation proxies if data allows[^9][^4]


### Computable from event data

- Pass counts
- Pass success
- Carry chains
- Recoveries
- Turnovers
- Shots
- Set pieces
- Duels/challenges
- Sequence starts and ends[^2][^6]


### Computable from both together

- Pass networks.
- Pressure around events.
- Rest-defense at turnover.
- Pitch control approximations.
- Dangerous transition windows.
- Expected possession value-like metrics.
- Sequence effectiveness.[^7][^1][^5]


## Important truth

Many football questions do **not** have one perfect metric.
They are answered through a **bundle of metrics + a visual + context**.[^2][^1]

For example:

“Were we too open?” is not one number.
It may require:

- width,
- length,
- line spacing,
- area occupied,
- opponent entries through central zones,
- and specific moments where spacing broke.[^1][^4]

So your system should not pretend every question maps to one KPI.
Instead, it should define an **answer package** for each question type.

## Best answer package model

For each coach/analyst question, the assistant should produce:

1. **Primary metric** — the main numeric proxy.
2. **Supporting metrics** — 2 to 4 numbers for context.
3. **Visual representation** — the clearest pitch/timeline/network view.
4. **Natural-language interpretation** — what it means in football terms.[^3][^2]

Example:

**Question:** “Did we counter-press well after losing the ball?”
**Answer package:**

- Primary metric: % of losses followed by defensive action within 5 seconds.
- Supporting metrics: recoveries within 8 seconds, opponent progression distance, shots conceded after losses.
- Visual: turnover map + transition timeline.
- Interpretation: “Counter-pressing was effective in central zones but weak on the right side.”[^5][^1]


## Good system design

Your intelligent tablet should internally maintain a library like this:


| Question family | Tactical meaning | Metrics | Visuals |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Shape | Structure and spacing | width, length, centroid, hull area | average positions, hull map [^4][^1] |
| Build-up | How team exits and progresses | pass links, progression distance, lanes used | pass network, progression map [^7][^2] |
| Pressing | Pressure and disruption | PPDA, recoveries, forced wide rate | pressure map, timeline [^6][^9] |
| Transition loss | What happens after losing it | loss zones, rest-defense, time to recovery | turnover map, snapshots [^5] |
| Chance creation | Quality of attack | shots, xG, key passes, box entries | shot map, key pass map [^6][^10] |
| Players | Role execution and impact | involvement, centrality, progressions, recoveries | player heatmap, action map [^8][^11] |

That is how the system “knows” how to answer a football question from numbers.

## The most important concept

What you need is a **football ontology + metric ontology**.

Meaning:

- football concepts: press, buildup, compactness, rest defense, progression, overload, transition;
- metric definitions: exactly how each concept is measured from data.[^3][^1]

Without that layer, you only have coordinates.
With that layer, you have a football intelligence system.[^2][^1]

## Shortest way to say it

If you want the simplest answer:

**You do not answer coach/analyst questions directly from raw coordinates. You answer them by converting football concepts into measurable proxies, then combining those proxies into visual and verbal explanations.**[^3][^1]
