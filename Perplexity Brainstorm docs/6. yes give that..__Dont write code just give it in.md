# yes give that..

Dont write code just give it in  normal form

Yes — here is the **normal-form mapping** you asked for.

The main idea is: a coach or analyst asks a football question, and the system answers it by translating that question into **tactical meaning**, then into **measurable metrics**, and then into a **visual + spoken explanation**.[^1][^2][^3]

A useful way to structure your system is:

**question → football meaning → what to measure → what to show → how to answer**[^4][^1]

## Setup and orientation

These questions establish who was on the pitch, what the shape was, and how the match should be framed before deeper analysis starts.[^2][^1]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Show me our lineup | Who played and for how long | Starters, substitutes, time on pitch, inferred roles from average positions | Lineup card, substitutions timeline, role labels [^1] | “These were your starters, these players came on here, and these are the inferred roles.” [^1] |
| Show me our shape | Team structure at a high level | Average player positions, team centroid, width, length, line spacing | Average position map, formation estimate [^2] | “You mainly held a 4-3-3 type structure, with the midfield line slightly narrow.” [^2] |
| Did our shape change? | Whether structure shifted across the match | Shape by half, by phase, by pre/post substitution windows | Side-by-side average positions, shape timeline | “Your base shape stayed similar, but the front line narrowed after the substitution.” |
| Show me substitutions | Match-state and structural changes | Sub minutes, players in/out, role changes after substitution | Substitution bar and before/after shape comparison | “This change happened at minute X and altered the right-side structure.” |

## In possession

These questions are about buildup, progression, circulation, and how the team attacked with the ball.[^5][^2]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| How did we build up? | How the team started possessions from the back | First-phase passes, buildup sequences, who received early, exit routes from defensive third | Pass network, buildup sequence map [^2][^6] | “You built mainly through the left center-back and pivot, with limited central progression.” |
| Show me our pass network | Main passing relationships | Pass counts between pairs, average positions, connection strength, centrality | Pass network [^7] | “Most of the circulation went through these three players, with the right side less connected.” |
| Which side did we progress through? | Direction of ball advancement | Progressions by left, center, right; forward pass distance; final-third entries by channel | Channel progression chart, pitch arrows | “Most progression came through the left half-space, not through the center.” |
| Were we too slow in possession? | Tempo and circulation speed | Possession duration, passes per sequence, speed of vertical progression, time to reach final third | Sequence-duration chart, progression timeline | “You kept the ball well, but progression into dangerous zones was slow.” |
| Did we find players between the lines? | Ability to access advanced space | Receptions between lines, body orientation proxy if available, next action after reception | Reception map and involvement chart | “You found the attacking midfielder there a few times, but not consistently.” |
| How did we enter the final third? | Mechanism of attack progression | Final-third entries via pass, carry, wide cross, switch, direct ball | Entry-type chart + event map | “Most entries came from wide progression, not central combinations.” |
| How did we enter the box? | Last-stage chance construction | Box entries, cutbacks, crosses, carries, through balls | Box-entry map | “You reached the box mostly through wide deliveries rather than central combinations.” |

## Out of possession

These questions focus on block shape, defensive structure, and pressing behavior.[^8][^5]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| What shape did we defend in? | Defensive block structure | Average out-of-possession positions, line heights, distances between units | Defensive shape map | “You defended in a mid-block with a fairly narrow midfield line.” |
| Were we compact enough? | Team spacing without the ball | Team length, team width, convex hull area, line spacing | Compactness timeline + shape snapshots [^9] | “You were compact centrally, but the width increased too much in wide defensive phases.” |
| Did we press high, mid, or low? | Pressing height | Defensive actions by zone, starting pressure line, opponent pass start locations | Pressure heatmap + zone summary [^10][^11] | “Most of your pressure came in a mid-block, not a high press.” |
| Did our press work? | Whether pressure disrupted the opponent | PPDA, advanced recoveries, forced backward passes, forced wide rate | PPDA chart + pressure map [^10][^12] | “The press forced wide play, but it didn’t consistently win the ball high.” |
| Were we open between the lines? | Interior defensive vulnerability | Defensive-midfield gap, midfield-forward gap, receptions conceded between lines | Line-spacing view + receptions map | “The biggest issue was the space between midfield and defense in these moments.” |
| Which side did they hurt us on? | Main opponent threat channel | Opposition entries, carries, passes, shots by side | Opposition attack map by flank | “Most danger came down your left side, especially after switches.” |

## Defensive transition: after losing the ball

These are some of the most important questions in your system because they connect attack failure to defensive danger.[^13][^14]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| What happened after we lost the ball? | Immediate consequence of turnovers | Loss locations, next 5–10 seconds, opponent progression distance, shots/entries conceded | Turnover map + consequence panel [^13] | “Most losses were harmless, but these right-side losses led to quick opponent progression.” |
| Show me our transitions after losing possession | Quality of defensive transition | Time to first defensive action, time to recover shape, number behind ball at loss | Transition timeline + shape snapshots [^13] | “You reacted quickly in central areas, but wide losses exposed your rest defense.” |
| Did we counter-press well? | Immediate pressure after losing the ball | Defensive action within 3–5 seconds, regain within 8 seconds, opponent forced backward rate | Counter-press map + success-rate summary | “Counter-pressing worked best in the middle third and less well in wide advanced areas.” |
| Where were our dangerous ball losses? | Which turnovers hurt the team | Turnover zone, opposition chance value after loss, recovery delay | Dangerous turnover map | “The most dangerous losses came in the left half-space near the final third.” |
| What was our rest defense? | Security structure behind the attack | Number behind ball, width of last line, central cover, distances between covering players | Rest-defense freeze frames [^13] | “Your rest defense was often too flat, leaving open space after long attacks broke down.” |
| Were we vulnerable after losing it in attack? | Exposure during attacking transition | Final-third losses, shape at loss, opponent counter speed | Filtered attacking-loss map | “Yes, especially when both fullbacks were advanced at the same time.” |

## Attacking transition: after winning the ball

These questions measure how the team attacked after recovering possession.[^14][^2]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| What happened after we won the ball? | Immediate attacking behavior after regains | Recovery location, time to next forward action, progression distance, shot/entry outcomes | Recovery-to-attack flow map | “Regains in midfield often led to forward play, but not many became shots.” |
| Did we counterattack well? | Efficiency of direct transitions | Time from regain to final-third entry, speed of progression, shot-ending transitions | Counterattack sequence summary | “You countered quickly from central recoveries but lacked support from wide regains.” |
| Where did we win it and what did we do next? | Value of recoveries by zone | Recovery zone, next event, sequence result | Recovery map with outcome coding | “High recoveries created more threat than deep recoveries.” |
| Which recoveries led to chances? | Transition chance creation | Recoveries followed by box entry, shot, or key pass | Recovery-to-chance chart | “These advanced recoveries were your main source of transition threat.” |

## Chance creation and finishing

These questions move from territorial control to actual danger.[^15][^10]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Did we create enough? | Quantity and quality of chances | Shots, xG, xA, box entries, key passes [^10][^15] | Shot map + summary cards | “You had enough volume, but most chances were low quality.” |
| How did we create chances? | Mechanism of chance creation | Pre-shot passes, carries, crosses, cutbacks, sequence type | Key-pass map + sequence breakdown | “Most of your chances came from wide delivery rather than central combinational play.” |
| Where did our shots come from? | Shot profile | Shot zones, body part if available, xG by zone | Shot map [^15] | “The shot profile was mostly outside the box, with limited central close-range chances.” |
| Which player created the most danger? | Creative contribution | Key passes, xA, involvement in high-value sequences | Player chance-creation view | “This player created the highest-value chances despite fewer touches.” |
| Which attacks actually became shots? | Attack efficiency | Final-third sequences ending in shots, sequence conversion rate | Sequence funnel | “You reached good areas often, but converted too few possessions into attempts.” |

## Opposition threat

These questions mirror your own attacking questions, but from the opponent’s side.[^16][^8]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| How did they create against us? | Main opposition attacking mechanisms | Their entries, shot origins, key pass types, transition vs settled attacks | Opposition shot map + attack summary | “They created mostly from quick transitions and wide deliveries.” |
| How did they break our press? | Press resistance pattern | Passes that beat first line, progression after pressure, receiving pockets | Pressure-break map | “They often escaped through the right center-back into the central midfield zone.” |
| Which player hurt us most? | Main opposition threat source | Involvement in dangerous sequences, key passes, carries, shots | Opposition player impact panel | “This player was central to most of their dangerous sequences.” |

## Set pieces

Set-piece questions are usually very concrete and easier to operationalize from event data.[^17][^2]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Show me our corners | Offensive set-piece patterns | Corner delivery zone, first contact zone, shot outcome | Corner map | “Most corners were aimed toward the near-post corridor.” |
| Show me their corners | Defensive set-piece exposure | Delivery target, free player zones, first contact against | Defensive-corner map | “They targeted the central six-yard area repeatedly.” |
| Were we organized on defensive corners? | Marking quality and structure | Marking distances, free runners, second-ball coverage | Set-piece freeze-frame sequence | “Your first line was stable, but second-ball coverage was weak.” |
| Which set pieces led to shots? | Direct set-piece threat | Set pieces followed by attempts, shot quality | Set-piece outcome chart | “Only these two corners resulted in shots, both from second phases.” |

## Players and roles

These questions are less about raw numbers and more about tactical function.[^7][^18]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| What role did this player actually play? | Functional role in the system | Average positions by phase, involvement zones, receiving map, passing map | Player role card + heatmap | “He behaved more like an inverted fullback than a traditional wide fullback.” |
| Was the pivot involved enough? | Midfield support function | Touches, passes received in build-up, centrality, between-line connections | Role involvement dashboard | “The pivot was available, but not used consistently as the first progression outlet.” |
| Did the winger hold width or come inside? | Positional behavior | Heatmap, average width, receptions by lane, carry direction | Winger movement map | “He spent more time inside, which reduced natural width on that side.” |
| Which player was our key connector? | Link between units | Network centrality, receptions, forward links, sequence involvement [^7] | Pass network + player importance card | “This player connected defense to midfield more than anyone else.” |

## Match changes and comparisons

These questions are critical because coaches and analysts both want to know what changed and when.[^1][^2]


| Question | Tactical meaning | What to measure | What to show | Spoken answer style |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| What changed after halftime? | Phase shift across halves | Shape, field tilt, pass network, chance profile, pressure metrics by half [^11] | First-half vs second-half comparison | “The second half became more direct, with less central control.” |
| What changed after the substitution? | Structural and functional impact of a personnel change | Before/after shape, passing links, progression routes, defensive spacing | Before/after comparison panel | “The substitution improved width but reduced central support.” |
| Compare our left and right side | Side asymmetry | Entries, losses, recoveries, progression, chance creation by side | Left-right split dashboard | “The left side progressed more; the right side was safer but less dangerous.” |
| Compare us and them in transition | Transition superiority | Transition counts, speed, entries, shots after regain/loss | Dual transition comparison | “They were more efficient than you after regains, even with fewer transition moments.” |

## Analyst-only internal questions

These are questions the system should ask internally even if the coach never says them out loud, because they help generate a better answer.[^3][^1]


| Internal analyst question | Why it matters | What to measure |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| What pattern repeated enough to matter? | Avoids overreacting to one isolated sequence | Frequency of similar sequences, recurring zones, recurring player links |
| Was this intentional or forced by the opponent? | Distinguishes strategy from emergency behavior | Context before event, pressure state, field position |
| What phase of play does this belong to? | Puts the event into proper tactical context | Sequence classification into build-up, transition, attacking play, set play [^2] |
| Did the structure break before the outcome? | Finds root cause rather than final symptom | Spacing, line distance, player availability before the event |
| What changed the most over time? | Helps explain momentum shifts | Metric deltas by minute block or phase |
| Which metric best explains the coach’s feeling? | Converts subjective football language into measurable evidence | Match the question to the strongest proxy metric set |

## Recommended spoken answer format

Your assistant should answer every question in the same simple structure:[^3][^1]

1. **What was asked** — “You asked about our transitions after losing the ball.”
2. **What the system measured** — “I checked loss locations, rest-defense shape, recovery time, and opponent progression after each turnover.”
3. **What it found** — “Most losses were stable, but losses on the right side exposed the back line.”
4. **What to explore next** — “You can compare this before and after the substitution, or isolate final-third losses.”

That keeps the system football-natural without sounding robotic.

## Best way to use this in your product

The system should maintain this mapping internally as a **question library**.
So when the coach asks something simple like “Were we too open after losing it?”, the system already knows:

- the tactical meaning,
- the metrics to compute,
- the visuals to render,
- and the way to explain it.[^4][^1]

That is what turns raw coordinates into a usable football intelligence assistant.[^9][^3]
