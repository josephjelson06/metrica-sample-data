Yes — I understand. You want the system to be **voice-led**, and for that to work well, the coach needs a **natural library of football questions** to ask, grouped the way a real coach thinks about a match: shape, buildup, defending, transitions, chances, set pieces, and players.[^1][^2][^3]

Also, your example question — “show me the attacking phase transitions when ball was lost” — is close, but a real coach would usually ask it in slightly more football-natural ways like “What happened after we lost the ball in attack?” or “Show me our rest defense when attacks broke down”.[^2][^3]

## Best question groups

A real post-match coach usually asks questions in this order:[^4][^5][^1]

1. Match setup and structure.
2. In possession.
3. Out of possession.
4. Transitions.
5. Chance creation and finishing.
6. Set pieces.
7. Individual players.
8. Game-state changes and substitutions.

That sequence fits how tactical analysis is often broken into phases of play such as build-up, attacking play, transition, and set play.[^2]

## 1. Setup questions

These are the first questions the coach asks to orient the discussion:[^5][^4]

- Show me our lineup.
- Show me who started and who came on.
- Show me our average positions.
- What shape did we have in possession?
- What shape did we have out of possession?
- Did our formation change across the match?
- Show me our substitutions and the moments they happened.
- Show me the opposition lineup and shape.[^1]

These are perfect for voice because they are short and direct.

## 2. In-possession questions

This is where the coach asks how the team built and progressed the ball.[^3][^2]

- How did we build from the back?
- Show me our buildup structure.
- Who was our main buildup player?
- Which side did we progress through most?
- Show me our pass network.
- Show me the pass network from 30 to 35 minutes.
- Show me the left-back to left-winger connection.
- Did we find the free player in midfield?
- How often did we reach the final third?
- How did we enter the box?
- Were we too slow in possession?
- Show me our attacking shape in settled possession.[^3][^1]


## 3. Out-of-possession questions

These focus on block shape, pressing, and defensive structure.[^1][^3]

- What shape did we defend in?
- Did we press high, mid, or low?
- Show me where we pressed them.
- Did we force them wide?
- Were we compact enough?
- Show me our team length and width.
- Where did they progress against us?
- Which side did they attack most?
- Show me where we won the ball back.
- Were we too open between the lines?
- Did our front line press together?
- Show me our defensive actions in our half.[^6][^1]


## 4. Transition questions

This is the category closest to what you were trying to say.[^2][^3]

### When we lost the ball

- What happened after we lost the ball in attack?
- Show me our transitions after losing possession.
- Where did we lose the ball most often?
- Which ball losses became dangerous against us?
- Show me our rest defense when attacks broke down.
- Did we counter-press well?
- How quickly did we recover shape after losing the ball?
- Which losses led to shots against?
- Show me our attacking turnovers in the final third.
- Were we vulnerable on one side after losing it?[^6][^1]


### When we won the ball

- What happened after we regained possession?
- Show me our counterattacks.
- Did we attack quickly enough after recoveries?
- Where did we win the ball and what did we do next?
- Which recoveries led to entries or shots?
- Did we use the space well after regaining it?[^3][^2]

These are much more coach-natural than “attacking phase transitions when ball was lost.”

## 5. Chance creation questions

These are about whether possession actually led to danger.[^1][^2]

- How did we create chances?
- Show me our shot map.
- Show me our key pass map.
- Where did our shots come from?
- Which player created the most danger?
- Did we create enough inside the box?
- Were our chances central or wide?
- Which passing patterns led to shots?
- Show me our best attacking sequences.
- Which zone produced our best chances?[^6][^1]


## 6. Opponent threat questions

A coach will also ask where the opponent hurt us.[^3][^1]

- How did they create against us?
- Show me their shot map.
- Which side caused us the most problems?
- How did they enter our box?
- Which players connected most for them?
- Show me their dangerous transitions.
- Show me their best attacking sequences.
- Did they overload one flank?
- How did they break our press?[^6][^1]


## 7. Set-piece questions

These are very common and very practical.[^2][^1]

- Show me our corners.
- Show me their corners.
- How did we defend corners?
- Show me our free kicks into the box.
- Show me their second corner.
- Were we organized on defensive set pieces?
- Who attacked the near post?
- Who was left free on set pieces?
- Did our marking hold on corners?
- Show me the set pieces that led to shots.[^1][^6]


## 8. Player questions

These help the coach evaluate roles and execution.[^7][^3]

- Show me our right-back’s actions.
- How involved was the pivot?
- Show me the winger’s touches.
- Did the striker stay too high?
- Show me the center-backs in possession.
- Which player received between the lines most?
- Which player covered the most ground?
- Who connected most with the fullbacks?
- Show me Player X before and after halftime.
- Did the substitute change our structure?[^8][^7]


## 9. Comparison questions

These are powerful because they help the coach ask “what changed?”.[^4][^5]

- Compare first half and second half.
- Compare before and after the substitution.
- Compare our left side and right side.
- Compare our buildup before and after minute 60.
- Compare our pressing in the two halves.
- Compare us and them in transition.
- Compare our shape with and without the ball.
- Compare our shot quality and theirs.[^2][^1]


## Best voice phrasing

For a voice-led system, the best coach questions are usually:[^9][^10]

- short,
- phase-based,
- specific enough to filter,
- but still natural.

So the best format is:

**Show me + object + team + time/filter**[^10]

Examples:

- Show me our lineup.
- Show me our pass network from 30 to 35.
- Show me our losses in the final third.
- Show me their dangerous transitions.
- Show me our shape after the substitution.
- Compare our first-half and second-half press.[^9][^10]


## Best starter set

If you want a realistic “starter pack” of voice questions for a coach, I’d use these 20:[^3][^1][^2]

- Show me our lineup.
- Show me our average positions.
- What shape did we have in possession?
- What shape did we have out of possession?
- Show me our pass network.
- Show me our buildup.
- Which side did we progress through most?
- Show me our final-third entries.
- Show me where we lost the ball.
- What happened after we lost the ball in attack?
- Show me our counter-press.
- Show me where we won the ball back.
- Show me our dangerous transitions.
- Show me our shot map.
- Show me the key passes before our shots.
- How did they create against us?
- Show me their dangerous transitions.
- Show me our corners.
- Show me their corners.
- Compare before and after the substitution.


## Important correction

Your system should not expect the coach to invent analytical wording. The assistant should help by **teaching the coach what kinds of questions are available** through suggested prompts grouped by football logic.[^11][^12]

So instead of a blank voice interface, it should surface categories like:

- Setup
- In possession
- Out of possession
- Transitions
- Chances
- Set pieces
- Players
- Comparisons[^2][^3]

That way the coach learns the system just by using it.

## Best way to think about your example

Instead of saying:

- “Show me the attacking phase transitions when ball was lost”

a coach-friendly version would be:

- “What happened when we lost the ball in attack?”
- “Show me our transitions after losing possession.”
- “Show me our final-third ball losses.”
- “Which attacking turnovers hurt us most?”[^1][^3][^2]

Those are the kinds of voice questions your assistant should be built around.
