# One more thing.. What all questions would an analyst ask?

Yes — and this is a very important distinction.

A **coach** usually asks decision-oriented questions like “Are we too open?” or “How did they hurt us?” while an **analyst** asks more diagnostic questions like “What pattern kept recurring?”, “In which phase did the structure break?”, and “What changed after minute 60?”.[^1][^2][^3]

So if your tablet is replacing the analyst too, it should know both:

- **coach-facing questions**, and
- **analyst-facing questions behind the scenes**.[^4][^3]


## How analysts think

Analysts usually break the match into phases, thirds, structures, patterns, and changes over time rather than jumping straight to conclusions.[^2][^1]
They ask questions to identify:

- what happened,
- where it happened,
- when it happened,
- who drove it,
- why it happened,
- and whether it was repeated enough to matter.[^1][^2]


## 1. Orientation questions

These are the first questions an analyst asks to structure the match.[^5][^2]

- What were both teams’ starting shapes?
- How did the shapes change in and out of possession?
- Who were the likely roles from the anonymized data?
- Were there substitutions or positional rotations?
- What was the likely game model of each team?
- Which team controlled territory early?
- Did the match state change the structure?[^5][^1]


## 2. In-possession analyst questions

These are more diagnostic than a coach’s “show me buildup” type request.[^3][^1]

- What was the buildup structure in the first phase?
- Which players formed the first line of buildup?
- How did the team progress from first third to middle third?
- Which lanes were used most: central, half-space, or wide?
- Which players were the real connectors in possession?
- Did the team create overloads in specific zones?
- Were player movements complementary or disconnected?
- Was the possession structure stable or improvised?
- Did the team progress through short circulation, direct play, or switches?
- What recurring passing patterns appeared?[^6][^1]


## 3. Out-of-possession analyst questions

These try to understand the defensive idea and whether it held.[^7][^4]

- What was the pressing structure?
- Was the team in a high press, mid-block, or low block?
- What triggered the press?
- Which passes triggered pressure?
- Did the front players press together?
- Were distances between lines compact enough?
- Where did the block get broken?
- Did the defense force play wide or allow central access?
- Which side or zone was repeatedly exposed?
- Was the defensive shape reactive or coordinated?[^4][^7]


## 4. Transition analyst questions

This is one of the biggest analyst categories.[^8][^3]

### After losing the ball

- Where were the ball losses happening?
- Which losses were dangerous and which were harmless?
- What was the team’s rest-defense shape at the moment of loss?
- Did the nearest players counter-press immediately?
- How long did it take to recover shape?
- Which losses led to direct progression by the opponent?
- Were we exposed more centrally or wide after losing possession?
- Did one player or zone repeatedly cause unstable losses?[^3][^1]


### After winning the ball

- Where were recoveries made?
- Did recoveries trigger immediate attacks or slow possessions?
- Which recoveries became box entries or shots?
- Was there enough support around the ball after regains?
- Did the team exploit disorganized opposition moments quickly enough?[^8][^1]


## 5. Pattern-detection questions

This is where analysts go deeper than coaches.[^9][^2]

- What repeated pattern kept occurring?
- Which behavior happened often enough to be considered a pattern?
- Was that pattern intentional or forced by the opponent?
- Which player movements created space repeatedly?
- Which opposition movement disrupted our structure repeatedly?
- Were there repeating combinations on one flank?
- Did the same weakness appear in multiple phases?
- Which sequences explain the game better than the raw stats do?[^2][^9]


## 6. Change-over-time questions

Analysts constantly ask what changed, when, and why.[^2][^3]

- What changed from the first half to the second half?
- What changed after the substitution?
- Did the pressing height drop as the match went on?
- Did the buildup shape change after an adjustment?
- When did control of territory shift?
- Which pattern stopped working, and when?
- What did the opponent change to solve our strengths?
- Did our behavior change because of scoreline, fatigue, or tactical adaptation?[^3][^2]


## 7. Opposition-analysis questions

Analysts often ask this even in self-analysis because meaning comes from interaction between both teams.[^4][^1]

- What was the opponent trying to do?
- Which players were key to their structure?
- How did they progress through us?
- How did they break our press?
- Which side did they target?
- How did they create overloads?
- What did they do after regaining the ball?
- Which of their patterns hurt us most?[^1][^4]


## 8. Player-role questions

These are less about raw performance and more about role function.[^10][^1]

- What role did this player actually perform?
- Was the player positioned as intended in each phase?
- Did the player connect the right units?
- Was the player available in the right spaces?
- Did the fullback invert, overlap, or stay?
- Did the pivot offer enough support angles?
- Did the winger hold width or attack inside?
- Did the striker pin the back line or drop too early?
- Which anonymous player behaved like the tactical hub?[^10][^1]


## 9. Chance and consequence questions

Analysts connect patterns to output.[^4][^3]

- Which possessions led to the best chances?
- Which ball progressions actually produced danger?
- Were the shots good chances or low-value attempts?
- Which turnovers directly caused shots against?
- Which attacking patterns produced entries but not shots?
- Did our structure create control without penetration?
- Did the opponent’s best chances come from settled attacks or transitions?[^3][^4]


## 10. Training and recommendation questions

This is where the analyst turns findings into action.[^11][^3]

- What should be repeated?
- What should be corrected?
- What should be trained this week?
- Which problem is structural versus execution-based?
- Which issue is most urgent?
- What adjustment would solve the biggest recurring problem?
- Which pattern can become a strength if reinforced?
- Which weakness needs tactical correction versus individual coaching?[^11][^3]


## Best way to use this in your system

Your assistant should treat analyst questions as the **internal reasoning layer** and coach questions as the **spoken user layer**.[^4][^3]
For example:


| Coach asks | Assistant internally asks |
| :-- | :-- |
| “Show me our buildup.” | What was the buildup structure, who formed the first line, which lanes were used, what patterns repeated? [^1][^6] |
| “Were we too open after losing it?” | Where did losses occur, what was rest defense shape, how quickly was compactness restored, which losses became dangerous? [^3][^1] |
| “Why did their left side hurt us?” | Which zones did they target, what combinations repeated, what defensive spacing failed, what changed over time? [^4][^2] |

That is how the assistant starts to feel intelligent: the coach asks simply, but the system reasons like an analyst.[^2][^3]

## Best grouped list

If you want the shortest useful answer, these are the main analyst question groups:[^1][^3]

- Structure.
- Build-up.
- Progression.
- Creation.
- Defensive block.
- Pressing.
- Defensive transitions.
- Attacking transitions.
- Repeated patterns.
- Changes over time.
- Player roles.
- Consequences and outputs.
- Training actions.

That is basically how an analyst turns a match into a tactical story.[^1][^2]
